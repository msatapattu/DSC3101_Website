One-way ANOVA

One-way ANOVA

  • One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) is a statistical method for testing for differences in the means of three or more groups.

  • Typically used when there is one independent variable or factor (treatment), and the goal is to investigate if variations or different levels of that factor (or treatment) have a measurable effect on a dependent variable.

  • When comparing the means of three or more groups, it can tell us if at least one pair of means is significantly different, but it can’t tell us which pair.

Assumptions

  • Normality - each sample is taken from a normally distributed population.

  • Independance - each sample has been drawn independently of the other samples.

  • Equal variance - variance of data in the different groups should be the same.

Hypotheses

  • For \(k\) means, we test the hypotheses,

\[H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \dots = \mu_k\] \[Vs\] \[H_1: \text{ At least two of the means are not equal}\]

where \(\mu_i\) is the mean of the \(i^{th}\) level of the factor.

Model for One-Way ANOVA

  • Each observation can be written in the form \[Y_{ij} = \mu_i + \varepsilon_{ij}\]

where \(\varepsilon_{ij}\) represents random error. (Same as the error term in the regression models.)

Checking Assumptions

Normality

The normality assumption can be checked by using one of the following two approaches:

  1. Analyzing the ANOVA model residuals to check the normality for all groups together. This approach is easier and it’s very handy when you have many groups or if there are few data points per group.

  2. Check normality for each group separately. This approach might be used when you have only a few groups and many data points per group.

Checking Normality in R

  1. By analyzing the model residuals.
  • Normality of residuals can be tested visually via a QQ-plot, and/or formally via a normality test (Shapiro-Wilk test).
  • Before checking the normality assumption, we first need to compute the ANOVA. Then use the residuals to check the normality.

Checking Normality in R

library(car)
qqPlot(one_way_anova$residuals,
  id = FALSE # id = FALSE to remove point identification
)

Checking Normality in R

  • Points in the QQ-plots roughly follow the straight line and most of them are within the confidence bands, also indicating that residuals follow approximately a normal distribution.
  • Here we checked normality assumption visually.
  • As a formal normality test, we can use Shapiro-Wilk test.

Shapiro-Wilk Test in R

  • Here we test the hypotheses,

\(H_0\): data come from a normal distribution \(\qquad Vs\)

\(H_1\) : data do not come from a normal distribution.

shapiro.test(one_way_anova$residuals)

    Shapiro-Wilk normality test

  • P-value > 0.05 and we do not reject \(H_0\) and conclude that the residuals follow approximately a normal distribution at \(5\%\) level of significance.

Checking Assumptions

Homogeneity of Variances

  • Now let’s check whether the variances are equal across species or not.
  • The result will have an impact on whether we use the ANOVA or the Welch ANOVA.
  • We can formally test for equality of the variances with a Levene’s or Bartlett’s test. Notice that the Levene’s test is less sensitive to departures from normal distribution than the Bartlett’s test.

Checking Homogeneity of variances

\(H_0\): variances are equal

\(\qquad Vs\)

\(H_1\): at least one variance is different.

Two-way ANOVA

Two-way ANOVA

  • Consider a situation in which it is desirable to investigate the effects of two factors that influence an outcome of an experiment.

  • For example, a teaching method (lecture, discussion,computer-assisted, etc.) and the size of a class might influence a student’s score on a standard test.

  • In such situations, the two-way ANOVA (two factorial design) is used to evaluate the effect of two different factors on a continuous outcome variable simultaneously.

Two-way ANOVA

  • To present general formulas for the analysis of variance of a two-factor experiment using repeated observations in a completely randomized design, we shall consider the case of \(n\) replications of the treatment combinations determined by \(a\) levels of factor \(A\) and \(b\) levels of factor \(B\).

  • The observations may be classified by means of a rectangular array where the rows represent the levels of factor \(A\) and the columns represent the levels of factor \(B\).

TWo-way ANOVA

  • Each treatment combination defines a cell in our array. Thus, we have \(ab\) cells, each cell containing \(n\) observations.

  • Denoting the \(k^{th}\) observation taken at the \(i^{th}\) level of factor \(A\) and the \(j^{th}\) level of factor \(B\) by \(y_{ijk}\), there are \(abn\) number of observations.

Two-factor experiment with \(n\) replications

Assumptions

  • The observations in the \((ij)^{th}\) cell constitute a random sample of size \(n\) from a population that is assumed to be normally distributed with mean \(\mu_{ij}\) and variance \(\sigma^2\).

  • All \(ab\) populations are assumed to have the same variance \(\sigma^2\).

  • Here we are assuming that the populations, where \(n\) independent identically distributed observations are taken, are combinations of factors.

Balanced and Unbalanced designs

  • When the sample sizes within cells are equal, it is a balanced design and the conventional two-way ANOVA test can be used in this circumstance.

  • When the sample sizes within each level of the independent variables are not the same, it is an unbalanced design and the ANOVA test should be handled differently.

Model for TWo-way ANOVA

  • Each observation in the table can be written in the form

\[y_{ijk}=\mu_{ij}+\varepsilon_{ijk},\]

where \(\varepsilon_{ijk}\) measures the deviations of the observed \(y_{ijk}\) values in the \((ij)^{th}\) cell from the population mean \(\mu_{ij}\).

  • Suppose, \(\alpha_i\) be the effect of the \(i^{th}\) level of factor \(A\), \(\beta_j\) be the effect of the \(j^{th}\) level of factor \(B\), \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}\) be the interaction effect of the \(i^{th}\) level of factor \(A\) and the \(j^{th}\) level of factor \(B\) and \(\mu\) be the overall mean.

Model for Two-way ANOVA

  • Then, we can write the model as,

\[y_{ijk}=\mu+ \alpha_i+ \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij}+ \varepsilon_{ijk}\] with the restrictions,

\[\sum_{i=1}^{a}\alpha_i=0, \qquad \sum_{j=1}^{b}\beta_j=0, \qquad \sum_{i=1}^{a}(\alpha\beta)_{ij}=0, \qquad \sum_{j=1}^{b}(\alpha\beta)_{ij}=0 \]

Hypotheses

The three hypotheses to be tested are as follows:

  1. \(H^A_0: \alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = \dots = \alpha_a = 0,\)

\(\quad H^A_1:\) At least one of the \(\alpha_i\) is not equal to zero.

  1. \(H^B_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \dots = \beta_b = 0,\)

\(\quad H^B_1:\) At least one of the \(\beta_j\) is not equal to zero.

  1. \(H^{AB}_0: (\alpha\beta)_{11} = (\alpha\beta)_{12} = \dots = (\alpha\beta)_{ab} = 0,\)

\(\quad H^{AB}_1:\) At least one of the \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}\) is not equal to zero.

Two-way ANOVA in R

  • It turns out that there is not just one way to calculate ANOVAs.

  • In fact, there are three different types - called,Type I, II and III.

  • These types differ in how they calculate variability (specifically the sums of of squares).

  • If the data is relatively balanced, then all three types will give you the same answer. If the data are unbalanced, then you need to use Type II or Type III.

Two-way ANOVA in R

  • The standard aov() function in base-R uses Type I sums of squares. Therefore, it is only appropriate when the data are balanced.

  • If the data are unbalanced, you should conduct an ANOVA with Type II or Type III sums of squares.

  • To do this, we can use the Anova() function in the car package. The Anova() function has an argument called type that allows you to specify the type of ANOVA you want to calculate.

Post-hoc tests in two-way anova

A significant two-way interaction indicates that the impact of one factor on the outcome variable depends on the level of the other factor. So, we can decompose a significant two-way interaction into:

  • Simple main effect: run one-way model of the first variable at each level of the second variable,

  • Simple pairwise comparisons: if the simple main effect is significant, run multiple pairwise comparisons to determine which groups are different.

Post-hoc tests in two-way anova

# Group the data by gender and fit  anova
model <- lm(score ~ gender * education_level, data = jobsatisfaction)
jobsatisfaction %>%
  group_by(gender) %>%
  anova_test(score ~ education_level, error = model)
